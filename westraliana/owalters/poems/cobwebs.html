First published March 26th 1948 in The Westralian Worker

COBWEBS were a great invention only twelve short months ago,
But the best — and worst — intention can become a source of woe.
And to-day poor Ross McLarty knows that he’s been sold a pup,
And is sorry that his party ever put the damned things up.

While the folk whose votes they wrangled are inquiring, “What the heck?”
Ross and Co. are badly tangled in a cobweb bottleneck.
They who promised speedy action in all matters, everywhere,
Plus the utmost satisfaction, now have cobwebs in their hair.

As Ross swings his duster bravely, Watts is lost in dust and doubt,
Cardell-Oliver says gravely, “We will never get them out.”
Parker sneezes, good old Nosey, Ross McDonald looks forlorn,
Seward picks a cobweb posy from a cobweb-festooned Thorn.

Doney doesn’t feel so able, Abbott doesn’t feel so good,
Peering ’neath a cobwebbed table where they last had sight of Wood.
So they struggle, so they bicker, as their first year o’er them rolls,
And the cobwebs grow still thicker, even in the pigeon-holes.

Cobwebs seemed a great invention when the voting went their way,
But they hate the very mention of the goddam things to-day.
Busy now with mop and duster, with their work not yet begun,
They can only rave and bluster, caught in cobwebs that they spun.

One of the Liberal Party's campaign slogans in 1947 was sweeping the Labor ‘cobwebs’ away; Labor had been in power in Western Australia for fourteen years at that point. The Coalition won the election, but were embarassed by the infighting that plagued their new government.
