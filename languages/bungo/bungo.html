What is Classical Japanese?

Classical Japanese (文語 (ぶんご), literally ‘refined language’) was the literary language of Japan for over a thousand years. It is largely based on the language of the mid-Heian period (平安時代(へいあんじだい)), the 10th and 11th centuries C.E., when spoken Japanese was in the stage known as Early Middle Japanese. Beginning with the following Kamakura period (鎌倉時代(かまくらじだい)) and over the following centuries, this Heian language remained the model that writers tried to imitate, even as the spoken language continued to evolve. Classical Japanese remained in use as a formal written language well into the 20th century, with it being used in official documents in particular until the end of the Second World War in 1945.

The earlier written language of the Nara period (奈良時代(ならじだい)), being derived from Old Japanese and found in early texts such as the Man'yōshū(万葉集(まんようしゅう)), displays some marked differences from the later language of the Heian period.
