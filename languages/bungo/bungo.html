What is Classical Japanese?

Classical Japanese (文語 (ぶんご), literally ‘refined language’) was the literary language of Japan for over a thousand years. It is largely based on the language of the mid-Heian period (平安時代(へいあんじだい)), the 10th and 11th centuries C.E., when spoken Japanese was in the stage known as Early Middle Japanese. Beginning with the following Kamakura period (鎌倉時代(かまくらじだい)) and over the following centuries, this Heian language remained the model that writers tried to imitate, even as the spoken language continued to evolve. Classical Japanese remained in use as a formal written language well into the 20th century, with it being used in official documents in particular until the end of the Second World War in 1945.

The earlier written language of the Nara period (奈良時代(ならじだい)), being derived from Old Japanese and found in early texts such as the Man'yōshū (万葉集(まんようしゅう)), displays some marked differences from the later language of the Heian period.

The Traditional Orthography of Japanese

The modern Japanese orthography was standardized in 1946, during the Allied Occupation period after the Second World War. The orthographic reforms of this time rendered modern Japanese spelling phonetic, apart from a handful of vestigial exceptions, and slightly simplified many of the 漢字 in common use in Japan.

The traditional orthography, known in Japanese as 歴史的仮名遣れきしてきかなづかい, “historical kana usage” or 旧仮名遣きゅうかなづかい, “old kana usage”, was a system that largely developed in the 平安時代, and, like modern English orthography, continued to reflect older pronunciations long after they had changed in contemporary speech. Compare for example the English spelling of “knight”, which is pronounced /naɪt/ in modern English, with the letter sequences “k” and “gh” silent, but was once pronounced something closer to /kniçt/, with all consonants sounded. Note also that the vowel has changed from a pure vowel to a diphthong in Modern English.

The Obsolete w-Row Kana

In the spelling reform of 1946, two kana of the w-row, whose consonants had become silent in modern pronunciation, were completely removed from the modern orthography. These were ゐ, ヰ wi and ゑ, ヱ we. In modern Japanese they are entirely replaced by い, イ i and え, エ e, but in the traditional orthography they occur normally in the words they were originally located in, as does を, ヲ wo, which similarly lost its initial -w and is retained in modern orthography only as the direct object marker.

The Standardization of Kana and the Distinction of mu and n

It was only in 1900 that the kana system itself was officially standardized in the modern hiragana and katakana forms, including the now obsolete characters for wi and we. The hiragana had been derived from cursive simplifications of 漢字. There are multiple ways to simplify characters, and multiple characters for any given Japanese sound, meaning that before 1900 there existed a great deal of variant kana forms in more or less common use. These non-standard variant forms are known as 変体仮名へんたいがな, and are still occasionally seen on store signs and in other decorative contexts. In contrast to the variation found in historical hiragana, the standard forms of katakana became regular rather early on, leading to little variation being found in historic katakana usage.

The moraic nasal sound, n /N/, did not exist in earlier forms of Japanese, and likely developed with the influence of Chinese loanwords. The kana system had already developed when the moraic nasal emerged, and the new sound was written with the same characters as the sound mu, relying on the reader understanding the context to distinguish the two sounds. For this reason the poem Iroha, which used all the sounds of Japanese at the time it was written, and was traditionally used as a kind of Japanese alphabet song, does not contain n. The two sounds were split and assigned separate characters, ん, ン for n and む, ム for mu, in the 1900 standardization of kana forms.
